Agentic AI vs a chatbot, what actually makes something "agentic"?

By Chaitali Mandavia November 2025 6 min read

"Agentic" is one of those words that shows up in every AI conversation right now, and most of the time it gets used loosely. Some people use it to mean any AI that does multiple steps. Some people use it to mean any AI with internet access. I think the actual distinction is simpler than both of those, and once you see it, the rest of the agentic AI vocabulary (tool use, orchestration, multi-step reasoning) starts to make sense as consequences of one core thing rather than separate features.

The one real difference

A chatbot's only output is text. Whatever it says back to you, you still have to go do it yourself. An agent's output is a changed world, an email actually sent, a calendar event actually created, a file actually edited. The core difference is acting versus advising.

a simple way to think about it

A chatbot is like asking someone for directions. They tell you turn left, then right, then it's the third building on your left. Useful, but you still have to drive there yourself. An agent is like asking someone to actually drive you. You give them the destination and they handle the rest, including reacting to a closed road or a wrong turn along the way.

Why everything else follows from this

Once an AI is allowed to actually act, a bunch of other things become necessary that simply weren't needed before. If a chatbot gives you a wrong answer, you read it, notice it's wrong, and move on. No harm done beyond a wasted moment. But if an agent takes a wrong action, an email gets sent to the wrong person, money gets moved to the wrong account, that's not a wasted moment anymore, that's a real consequence.

This is why agents need multi-step reasoning: they have to check the result of each action before deciding what to do next, the same way a person would, rather than just executing a fixed plan blindly. Multi-step reasoning isn't really a separate capability bolted onto agents. It's the machinery required for acting safely once advising is no longer the only thing on the table.

chatbot vs agent, the actual loop each one runs

The cookbook and the chef

Here's a second analogy that gets at a slightly different part of this. Think about the difference between a cookbook and a chef. A cookbook gives you a fixed recipe, two cups of flour, bake at 350 for twenty minutes, and it's the same instructions every single time regardless of what's actually happening in your kitchen.

A chef, on the other hand, is handed a goal (make something good with what's in this kitchen) and makes judgment calls the whole way through. How much salt depends on how the dish tastes right now. When the onions are ready depends on how they actually look, not a fixed timer. A good chef adjusts for what's actually happening, not what a static set of instructions predicted would happen.

Cookbook
fixed instructions, no judgment
Same steps every time, regardless of outcome
No way to react if something goes differently than expected
You still have to be the one cooking
Chef
a goal, then judgment all the way through
Adjusts based on what's actually happening
Notices when something's off and corrects course
Produces the finished dish, not instructions for one

A regular LLM prompt is the cookbook, a fixed set of instructions that gets followed once. An agent is closer to the chef, given a goal and the tools to actually carry it out, adjusting at each step based on what it's actually seeing.

Where you've actually seen this

This isn't just theoretical, there are real tools doing this right now.

Claude Code Doesn't just suggest code, it writes files, runs commands, checks if tests pass, and fixes things when they don't, all without you copying and pasting anything yourself.
Browser agents Tools that can navigate an actual website, click buttons, fill out forms, and complete a task like booking something or filling out a form, rather than just describing how you'd do it.
Research agents Given a question, they search multiple sources, read through them, decide what's actually relevant, and keep digging until they have a real answer, instead of answering from memory after one search.
the practical takeaway

When you hear "agentic" attached to a product, the one question worth asking is simple: does this thing actually change something in the world, or does it just tell you what to do next? If it's the second one, it might still be useful, but it's not really agentic, it's just a chatbot with extra steps.
