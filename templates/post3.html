Fundamentals

What is RAG, and what's actually happening underneath it?

By Chaitali Mandavia August 2025 7 min read

RAG stands for Retrieval Augmented Generation. The short version is that it optimizes the model's output by pulling in outside sources before it answers. That's the definition you'll find everywhere. But I think the more useful thing is to actually trace through what's happening mechanically, because once you see it, a lot of other AI buzzwords start making more sense too.

The problem RAG is solving

Say you have 500 documents and you want to ask a question about something specific in them. There are three ways you could try to do this.

1 Paste all 500 documents into the prompt. This doesn't work, they're way bigger than the model's context window (the thing I wrote about in my last post), and even if they did fit, it would be slow and expensive to process all of that every single time you ask a question.
2 Fine-tune a model on those 500 documents so it "memorizes" them. This is possible, but it's expensive, slow to update whenever the documents change, and the model can still get details wrong when reciting from memory, the same hallucination problem I covered before.
3 Do what RAG actually does. Don't put all 500 documents in. Instead, search for the few documents, or really the few specific paragraphs, that are most relevant to this particular question, and only put those into the context window.
1. You ask: "what does the contract say about termination clauses?"
2. A search system looks through all 500 documents
3. It finds the 3 most relevant paragraphs (say, from documents #12, #47, #203)
4. Those 3 paragraphs get added to the context window, along with your question
5. Now the model answers, but it's only looking at those 3 paragraphs, not all 500 documents

The model never "knows" about document #47 in some general sense. It only ever sees the specific chunk that got pulled in for this specific question.

a version of this you've probably already used

When you upload a file to ChatGPT or Claude, this is exactly what's happening behind the scenes. The model was not trained on your file, it has never seen it before. The moment you upload it, the file gets chunked and converted into coordinates, and every question you ask afterward gets matched against those chunks before the model answers. Without the upload, the model is only answering from what it learned during training. With the upload, it's also answering from what you just showed it.

Why everything gets chopped into chunks first

Before any of this can happen, every document gets broken into smaller pieces, usually called chunks. The reason is that a document might be 50 pages long, and the part that actually answers your question might be one paragraph buried on page 32. If the system retrieved the whole document every time, you'd be right back to the original problem of stuffing too much into the context window.

So as a setup step, before anyone ever asks a question, every document gets split apart:

Document #47 (50 pages) gets split into:
  Chunk 1: paragraph 1-2
  Chunk 2: paragraph 3-4
  Chunk 3: paragraph 5-6
  ... maybe 40 chunks total

Same thing happens to all 500 documents
→ you end up with maybe 20,000 chunks total

Now when you ask a question, the system isn't searching whole documents anymore, it's searching across all 20,000 small chunks and pulling out the handful that actually matter. Those are the 3 paragraphs from the example above.

How does it know which chunks are relevant?

This is the part that confused me the most when I first understood the rest of it. The system isn't doing something like Ctrl+F, a keyword search. And it's clearly not having the LLM "read" all 20,000 chunks every time, that would be just as slow as the original problem.

What actually happens is that every chunk gets converted into a list of numbers, called an embedding, before any question is ever asked. Think of it like a coordinate on a map. Chunks that are about similar topics end up near each other on that map, and chunks about completely different topics end up far apart.

Chunk about "termination clauses"    → [0.2, -0.8, 1.4, ...]
Chunk about "termination procedures" → [0.3, -0.7, 1.3, ...]  ← close by
Chunk about "vendor payment terms"      → [-1.1, 0.9, -0.4, ...] ← far away

This conversion happens once, ahead of time, for all 20,000 chunks. It's a one-time cost that you pay when you set up the system, not something that happens every time someone asks a question.

When you actually do ask a question, your question gets converted into the same kind of coordinate. Then the system does something much simpler than "understanding" 20,000 chunks: it just does math. It computes the distance between your question's coordinate and every chunk's coordinate, and picks the closest few.

why this is fast

Comparing coordinates is just arithmetic, not comprehension. A computer can do this across millions of coordinates in milliseconds. It's nothing like an LLM having to actually read and reason about 20,000 documents, it's closer to looking up the nearest pin on a map. The expensive, slow part, the LLM actually reading and thinking, only happens on the 3 chunks that won at the end.

Why this matters beyond just RAG

Once this clicks, a bunch of other AI vocabulary starts making more sense too. A vector database is just a database optimized for storing and searching these coordinates quickly. Embeddings are the coordinates themselves. And RAG is really just a pattern, search first, then generate, that shows up everywhere once you start looking for it.
