AI-native, AI-enabled, AI-first — what do these actually mean?

By Chaitali Mandavia June 2025 6 min read

There is so much you can do with AI tools once you understand what people are actually saying. The problem is that a lot of the language around AI gets thrown around in pitch decks and company announcements without anyone stopping to explain it. AI is not just a tech thing anymore, you will see its impact in healthcare, finance, law, education, almost any industry. So it's worth knowing what these words mean.

Let's start with the two that get mixed up the most.

AI-enabled vs AI-native

These sound similar but describe pretty different things. The easiest way to tell them apart is to ask one question: if you removed the AI, would the company or product still exist?

the core distinction
AI-enabled
existing process, AI does part of it
The workflow was designed for humans first
AI speeds up or improves one step
Remove the AI, the process still works
Example: a law firm using AI to summarize documents
AI-native
AI is the process, not a layer on top
Built around AI from day one
The product couldn't exist without AI at the center
Remove the AI, there is no product
Example: Cursor, Perplexity, GitHub Copilot

A recruitment firm that used to screen CVs manually and now uses an AI tool to do the first filter is AI-enabled. The process is the same, the AI just does one step faster. A company like Perplexity, which is essentially a search engine that generates answers instead of returning links, is AI-native. The whole product is built around the model. You can't separate the two.

What about AI-first?

When a company is making decisions, whether about hiring, building a new feature, or redesigning a process, AI is the first thing they reach for rather than the last. It doesn't mean everything is automated, it means the default assumption is "can AI do some version of this?" before adding a human workflow.

an analogy that could help

In the 2010s, companies went "mobile-first." It meant that when they designed something new, they started with the phone experience instead of the desktop and worked outward. AI-first is the same shift where companies start with what AI can do, then figure out where humans add the most value on top.

The buzzword glossary

These are terms you will keep hearing. Here is what they actually mean and how much weight to give them.

AI-native
A company or product built from the ground up with AI at the center. The product wouldn't function without it. Cursor, Perplexity, and Harvey (legal AI) are examples.
AI-enabled
An existing business or process that has added AI to one or more steps. Most enterprise AI adoption right now falls here.
AI-first
Means AI is the default consideration when building or redesigning anything. Often used in company strategy decks.
AI at the core
Another way of saying AI-native, usually used in marketing. The phrase itself tells you nothing. The question is always what specifically the AI is doing and whether the product works without it.
Digital transformation
The older version of this conversation, from the 2010s. Meant moving processes from paper or legacy systems to digital ones. Now it often gets recycled to mean AI adoption. If someone uses this to describe an AI project, they may be repackaging old thinking.
Agentic AI
AI that doesn't just respond to a single prompt but takes a sequence of actions to complete a task. It can browse the web, run code, call APIs, and make decisions along the way. This is where things get genuinely different from a chatbot. I cover this in a separate post.
Foundation model
The large underlying model trained by companies like OpenAI or Anthropic that everything else is built on top of. GPT-4 and Claude are foundation models. Most "AI products" are really a thin layer built on someone else's foundation model. Understanding this matters when evaluating whether a company has a real technical moat.
