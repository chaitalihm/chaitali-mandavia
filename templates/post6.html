Technical Deep Dive

The actual math behind why batch size matters

By Chaitali Mandavia 2025 13 min read

In my last post I said more tokens means more compute, roughly proportional, and that the relationship comes from something called arithmetic intensity. This post is the follow-up for anyone who wants to actually see the math, not just take my word for it. We're going to walk through the real derivation, the same one used in production engineering to decide how big a batch needs to be before a chip is actually being used efficiently.

Three things that determine how fast anything runs

Whenever a model runs on hardware, three numbers determine how long it takes. How fast the chip can do math, measured in floating point operations per second, or FLOPs/s. How fast the chip can move data between memory and its compute cores, measured in bytes/s, called bandwidth. And how much total memory is available to store that data.

For a single matrix multiplication, this comes down to two competing timers running at once: the time spent doing the actual math, and the time spent moving data in and out. Whichever one takes longer is your real bottleneck.

Two different "intensities" that get compared against each other

Here's a distinction that's easy to blur together, and getting it clear makes everything after this much easier to follow. There are actually two separate numbers in this story, both called "intensity," but they mean different things.

The first is the hardware's intensity. This is a fixed property of a specific chip, it has nothing to do with what computation you're running. It just asks: for this chip, how many math operations can it do in the time it takes to fetch one byte from memory? You get it by dividing two numbers straight off the hardware's spec sheet.

Hardware Intensity = Peak FLOPs/s ÷ Peak Bandwidth (bytes/s) For a TPU v5e: 1.97e14 FLOPs/s ÷ 8.2e11 bytes/s = 240 FLOPs/byte

That 1.97e14 and 8.2e11 are real, published numbers straight from the TPU v5e's spec sheet, but the 240 itself is something the rooflines paper's authors calculated themselves by dividing the two, not a number any hardware vendor ever published directly.

The second is the algorithm's intensity. This is a property of the specific computation you're running, not the chip. It asks: for this particular matrix multiplication, how many math operations am I actually doing per byte I actually have to load? This number changes depending on the size of the matrices you're multiplying.

Algorithm Intensity = Total FLOPs the matmul does ÷ Total bytes the matmul loads

The whole point of this post is to work out what that algorithm intensity formula actually looks like for a real matrix multiplication, and then compare it against the fixed 240 from the hardware. 240 isn't a ceiling the chip can't go past, it's the minimum you want to clear. Below 240, the chip's math units are sitting idle waiting on bytes to arrive from memory. Above 240, the math units are fully busy, memory isn't holding anything back anymore, and the chip just keeps doing more math as batch size keeps growing. You want to be above 240, the higher the better.

Working out the algorithm's intensity for matrix multiplication

Matrix multiplication, the operation that makes up the vast majority of what a transformer actually does, is the case worth working through in detail. Say we're multiplying X, shaped [B, D], by Y, shaped [D, F], to get a result Z, shaped [B, F].

a matrix multiplication, animated

Worked through the same way we did for the dot product earlier, the FLOPs and bytes for this operation come out to:

the derivation
Bytes loaded for X: 2BD  (B×D numbers, 2 bytes each)
Bytes loaded for Y: 2DF  (D×F numbers, 2 bytes each)
Bytes written for Z: 2BF

Total FLOPs: 2BDF

Intensity = 2BDF / (2BD + 2DF + 2BF)

In a real transformer, D and F are large, often 8,000 or more, while B, the number of tokens being processed at once, is usually much smaller. When that's true, the BD and BF terms in the denominator are tiny next to DF, small enough to drop entirely without meaningfully changing the answer. That leaves:

Intensity ≈ 2BDF / 2DF = B

The whole thing collapses down to just the batch size. This is the cleanest result in the entire framework: how efficiently your hardware runs is, to a first approximation, just a function of how many tokens you're processing at once.

Comparing the algorithm's intensity against the hardware's

We now have both numbers we need. The hardware's fixed intensity is 240, the algorithm's intensity (for a matmul, once you simplify it) is approximately B. The rule comes out of just comparing the two:

B > 240  → compute-bound, hardware running efficiently B < 240  → memory-bound, hardware sitting idle waiting on data

This is part of why batching matters so much in production AI systems. If you process one user's request at a time (B is small), you're almost certainly memory-bound, leaving most of the chip's FLOPs capacity unused. If a serving system can batch many requests together before running them through the model (pushing B above 240), it suddenly gets much better use out of the same hardware.

the crossover, visualized

The int8 version, and why the rule barely changes

What "quantizing" actually means

Before working through int8, it's worth defining the term plainly. Quantizing just means storing the same numbers using fewer bytes. A model's weights normally live in bfloat16, 2 bytes per number. Quantizing to int8 means rounding those same numbers down so each one fits in just 1 byte. You lose a little precision in exchange for a smaller memory footprint, and as we're about to see, that tradeoff has a direct, calculable effect on arithmetic intensity.

One more piece worth covering, since it shows up constantly in deployment decisions. If you store your weights in int8 instead of bfloat16, you cut the bytes for Y in half, but int8 hardware also runs at roughly double the FLOPs/s. Working through the same derivation with int8 numbers (3.94e14 FLOPs/s, 8.2e11 bytes/s):

int8 derivation
Algorithm Intensity ≈ 2B  (doubled, since bytes were halved)
Hardware Intensity = 3.94e14 / 8.2e11 = 480  (also doubled)

B > 480 / 2 = 240

The two effects exactly cancel out. Quantizing to int8 doubles your algorithm's intensity, but it also doubles the hardware's threshold, so the minimum batch size needed to be compute-bound stays exactly the same, 240.

What int8 quantization buys you isn't a lower batch-size requirement, it's more raw speed once you clear that same threshold, and this part comes from a separate fact, not from the intensity math at all. The chip's int8 FLOPs/s (3.94e14) is simply double its bfloat16 FLOPs/s (1.97e14), a published hardware fact about how int8 circuitry is built, not something derived from intensity. So once you're compute-bound in either format, holding batch size fixed, the int8 version finishes twice as much math in the same second, purely because the chip's int8 hardware can do twice the operations per second by design.

What "inference" means, and why it's harder to keep efficient

Inference is just the term for actually using a trained model to get an answer, as opposed to training, which is the process of teaching the model in the first place. When you type a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude, that's inference. Training is a separate, earlier process where the model's weights get adjusted over and over against huge amounts of data.

The reason this distinction matters for everything we've just worked through: during training, engineers deliberately choose large batch sizes, often in the thousands of tokens, specifically to stay comfortably above that 240 threshold and keep the chip compute-bound. During inference, especially when it's a single person typing a single prompt, the natural batch size can be as low as 1, far under 240. That means a huge fraction of the chip's FLOPs capacity sits idle, waiting for the next byte to arrive, every single time someone uses the model.

why training and inference sit on opposite sides of the crossover

What "batch size tuning" means in practice

Batch size tuning is the work of deciding how many requests, or tokens, to process together in a single pass through the model rather than one at a time. A bigger batch means more math per byte loaded, which pushes the algorithm's intensity closer to, or past, that 240 threshold. The tradeoff a team has to balance is that a larger batch also takes a bit longer to fill up before it can start running, so tuning means finding a batch size that keeps the hardware efficient without making any individual user wait too long for a response.

What "continuous batching" means

This is the actual technique that makes high batch sizes possible in a live, real-world serving system, where requests don't arrive neatly all at once. Instead of waiting to collect a full batch before starting, a server using continuous batching keeps slotting new incoming requests into a batch that's already running, as older requests in that same batch finish up. This keeps the effective batch size high almost all the time, without forcing any single user to sit and wait for a batch to fill up first.

continuous batching, requests join and leave as they're ready

Every additional request a system can fit into a running batch is essentially free throughput, since it's using compute capacity that would otherwise sit idle waiting on memory. This is why continuous batching exists as its own area of engineering effort inside companies that serve LLMs at scale.

What's actually being done to make this more efficient, and what it means for you

The compute-bound versus memory-bound tradeoff we've worked through is one of the central constraints AI infrastructure teams are working against right now, and most of the meaningful efficiency gains in the field are direct attacks on one side or the other of this exact ratio.

A few of the real directions people are working on: mixture-of-experts models, where only a fraction of the model's total parameters get used for any given token, which shrinks the bytes that need loading without shrinking the model's overall capacity. Better quantization formats, going past int8 into even lower precision while finding ways to preserve quality, since every halving of bytes pushes intensity up for free. Speculative decoding, where a small, cheap model guesses several tokens ahead and a larger model just verifies them, which effectively raises the batch size during the part of inference that's normally hardest to keep efficient, generating tokens one at a time. And smarter serving systems, continuous batching being one example, that squeeze real-world traffic closer to that 240 threshold even when requests arrive unpredictably.

As someone actually using these models day to day rather than building the infrastructure behind them, the most direct way this affects you is through token count, which is the one lever you have control over. None of the batching or hardware optimization happens on your end, that's entirely the provider's problem to solve. But every token you send, in your prompt or in the response you ask for, is still doing real, measurable work on a real chip somewhere, and that's reflected directly in what you're charged per token. A long, repetitive prompt costs more than a tight one for the same reason a model takes longer to generate a long response than a short one, more tokens simply means more passes through the exact math we just walked through.

tying it all together

The whole chain of reasoning starts from one simple ratio, FLOPs divided by bytes, and ends up explaining real decisions: why a provider quantizes its models to int8, why training and inference are efficient in such different ways, why serving teams build continuous batching systems, and why API pricing tends to favor high-volume customers who can keep batch sizes consistently high. A provider serving millions of requests can batch aggressively and stay compute-bound most of the time. A low-volume deployment, or one with bursty, unpredictable traffic, struggles to keep its effective batch size above 240 consistently, which is part of why dedicated capacity or reserved throughput gets priced differently than simple pay-per-token access.

This entire derivation follows the rooflines framework laid out in "How To Scale Your Model," Austin et al., Google DeepMind, 2025, which goes considerably further into network communication rooflines across multiple chips, tile sizes, and several worked problems if you want to go deeper than this post does.
