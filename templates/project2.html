Deploying agentic AI for the semiconductor team at a long/short hedge fund

Stage 1

Why this makes economic sense

The semiconductor team covers a lot of companies across a fast-moving market, and the problem is not that the analysts are bad at their jobs but that there is genuinely too much to read and not enough hours in the day to read all of it.

the pitch

A long/short fund lives on information speed, and when TSMC revises its capacity guidance or ASML gets hit by a new export restriction, the team that knows first is the one that positions first. Right now the semiconductor analysts spend most of their time gathering and summarizing rather than actually deciding anything, and the goal is to build something that handles that first part so they can spend their time on the second.

Larger funds like Millennium and Point72 have been building AI research infrastructure for a while now, and Bloomberg and FactSet both launched LLM-based research tools in 2024. The real question for a mid-size fund is whether off-the-shelf tools are enough, and my view is that they are not. Generic tools cover general market news fine but they don't understand something like the relationship between ASML shipment delays and TSMC's 3nm yield trajectory, and that kind of specificity only comes from something built for this team and this coverage universe.

what I would recommend

Build a custom agentic monitoring system for the semiconductor team specifically. Start with the top 15 holdings, get it working well there, then expand. Going broad too early is how these projects fall apart.

Stage 2

Questions I'd ask before building anything

The number one reason AI projects fail is that nobody spent enough time with the actual users before building. I would want to sit down with the analysts and the CIO before touching any code. These are the questions I would ask.

Coverage and data
1What does your coverage universe look like? Full list, how you'd bucket them by importance, and which 10 companies, if you missed something on them, would hurt the most.
2Where does your information come from today? Every source an analyst touches in a typical week: terminals, filings, supply chain data providers, broker reports, export control filings.
3What do you have API access to, and what requires a human to go find it?
Workflow and pain points
4Walk me through a typical Monday morning. What do you read, in what order, and what do you produce by end of day?
5What's the most time-consuming thing that adds the least value? Synthesizing earnings transcripts? Tracking supply chain signals? Writing first drafts of research notes?
6Tell me about a specific time you were late on something that mattered. What happened, and what would have needed to be different?
7What signals have historically driven your best trades? Capacity utilization shifts? Lead time changes? Export license approvals? Management tone on earnings calls?
Technical and org
8What are your data residency and compliance requirements? Restrictions on sending information to third-party APIs?
9Where would analysts actually want to see this? In their existing tools, a new dashboard, a Slack digest, email?
10Who owns the decision to adopt this? Who has veto power?
Stage 3

What the system actually looks like

An orchestrator agent runs on a schedule, pulls from multiple sources, extracts what matters, and routes it to the right analyst with enough context to act on it. No voice, no computer vision needed here, this is fundamentally a text-heavy information synthesis problem.

how the system is structured
why agentic and what it actually does

The reason this needs to be agentic rather than a simpler RAG pipeline is that monitoring 50+ companies is not a retrieval problem, it is a reasoning problem. The system needs to decide what to look for across multiple sources, figure out when something warrants digging deeper, and prioritize signals across the whole coverage universe simultaneously. A static pipeline can only return what you ask for. An agent figures out what to ask for in the first place.

In practice the agent would be calling a set of tools on each run: web search and news APIs to pull the latest on each company, a document reader to process new filings, a vector store lookup to retrieve relevant historical context, a signal scoring function to rank what actually matters, and a drafting step to put together the analyst briefing. The human analyst is always the final step before anything reaches the PM.

The one thing I would insist on: start with the top 15 holdings only. Trying to cover 50+ companies from day one is how these projects fall apart before they prove anything.

Stage 4

How you'd know if it's working

You need to decide what success looks like before the pilot starts. If you wait until after, it's too easy to rationalize any result. These are the metrics I'd lock in upfront with targets everyone agrees to.

pilot structure
how the pilot would run

The pilot would run for 60 days with 2 analysts covering the top 15 holdings. Both analysts keep doing their normal workflow alongside the system the whole time so you have something to actually compare against. There's a check-in at day 30 to tune anything that needs tuning, and a go or no-go decision at day 60 based on the KPIs.

The one thing I would insist on before any of this starts is a baseline week where analysts track exactly how they spend their time. Without that number, the 40% reduction target is just a guess and nobody can actually verify whether it happened.

Stage 5

Getting it actually used

Working on ALMA taught me that the technical build is the easier part. Getting people to trust it and actually change how they work is where most of the effort goes.

Month 1-2
Pilot with 2 analysts on the top 15 holdings
System runs alongside the existing workflow. Weekly feedback sessions to tune signal scoring based on what analysts are actually dismissing as noise.
Month 3
Review what worked, fix what didn't, bring in the full team
Go through the KPIs honestly. Fix the top issues. Onboard the rest of the analysts with a hands-on session using their actual companies, not a slide deck.
Month 4
Expand to the full 50+ company universe
Only if the pilot KPIs were actually met. New data sources like export control filings and Tier 2/3 supply chain data get added here.
Month 5-6
Monitor model health
The semiconductor landscape shifts constantly. Assign one analyst as the internal owner whose job is to flag when outputs start degrading before the rest of the team notices.
Month 6+
Report back to leadership on actual ROI
A simple dashboard showing time saved, signal recall, and any cases where the system surfaced something before a market move. This is how the project gets renewed.
risks worth flagging upfront

The biggest thing to get right upfront is analyst buy-in. Senior analysts may feel a tool like this threatens their role, and if that perception takes hold it doesn't matter how good the outputs are. Being explicit that this is about giving them more time for the work that actually matters, and getting one or two of them involved in shaping what the output looks like early on, goes a long way. People trust things they had a hand in building.

Hallucination is the other major risk and it's especially serious in a financial context. LLMs can state incorrect numbers with complete confidence, which is something we ran into building ALMA too. Every figure in the output needs to be traced back to a specific source, and the system needs guardrails that force it to verify its own claims before anything goes out to an analyst.

A few other things worth sorting out early: data licensing, because some providers prohibit their content from being fed into AI models and legal needs to review everything before ingestion; model drift, because the semiconductor landscape moves fast and someone needs to own a quarterly check on whether the outputs still reflect reality; and over-reliance, which you address simply by always including source links and tracking whether analysts are actually clicking through to them.
