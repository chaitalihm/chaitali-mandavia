Deploying agentic AI at a long/short semiconductor fund

Stage 1

Why this makes economic sense

Before writing a line of code, you need to show the fund why this is worth doing. Semiconductor markets are a good fit for this argument because the coverage universe is wide, information moves fast, and analyst time is genuinely the bottleneck.

50+
companies to track across TSMC, ASML, Nvidia, SMIC, and the broader supply chain
~3 hrs
analyst time per company per week just to stay current on filings, news, and supply chain signals
$22k
weekly labor cost across the team at roughly $150/hr — before any AI investment
the pitch

A long/short semiconductor fund lives on information speed. When TSMC revises its capacity guidance, when ASML gets hit by a new export restriction, when a supplier in Malaysia goes offline — the fund that knows first positions first. Right now analysts spend most of their time gathering and summarizing information rather than actually doing something with it.

The idea is to give the team a system that reads everything, flags what matters, and puts together a first draft so analysts can focus on the stuff that actually needs a human. Not to replace anyone, just to clear the noise.

what others are doing

Larger funds like Millennium and Point72 have been investing in AI research infrastructure for a while. Bloomberg and FactSet both launched LLM-based research tools in 2024. The question for a mid-size fund is whether off-the-shelf tools are enough or whether a custom build makes more sense.

Generic tools cover general market news fine, but they don't understand the relationship between something like ASML shipment delays and TSMC's 3nm yield trajectory. That kind of domain-specific signal is only possible with something purpose-built for this coverage universe.

what I would recommend

Build a custom agentic monitoring system designed around the semiconductor supply chain specifically. Start with the top 15 holdings, prove it out, then expand. Going broad too early is how these projects fall apart.

Stage 2

Questions I'd ask before building anything

The number one reason AI deployments fail is that nobody spent enough time understanding the actual workflow before building anything. I would want to sit down with the analysts and the CIO before touching any code.

Coverage and data
1What does your coverage universe actually look like? Give me the full list, how you'd bucket them by importance, and which 10 companies, if you missed something on them, would cost you the most.
2Where does your information come from today? Walk me through every source an analyst touches in a typical week — terminals, filings, supply chain data providers, export control filings, broker reports.
3What data do you have API access to, and what requires a human to go find it?
Workflow and pain points
4Walk me through what an analyst does on a typical Monday morning. What do they read, in what order, and what do they produce by end of day?
5What's the most time-consuming thing analysts do that adds the least value? Synthesizing earnings transcripts? Tracking supply chain signals? Writing first drafts?
6Can you tell me about a specific time you were late on something that mattered? What happened, and what would have needed to be different?
7What signals have historically driven your best trades? Capacity utilization shifts? Lead time changes? Export license approvals? Management tone on calls?
Technical and compliance
8What are your data residency and compliance requirements? Does firm data need to stay in a specific region? Any restrictions on sending information to third-party APIs?
9Where would analysts want to see AI output? In their existing tools, a new dashboard, a Slack digest, email?
10Who owns the decision to adopt this internally? Who has veto power?
Stage 3

What the system actually looks like

The core idea is an orchestrator agent that runs on a schedule, pulls from multiple data sources, extracts structured signals, and routes them to the right analyst with context. No voice, no computer vision — this is a text-heavy information synthesis problem.

key decisions

Why agentic and not a simple RAG pipeline? Monitoring 50+ companies requires the system to decide what to look for, when to dig deeper on a signal, and how to prioritize across the whole coverage universe at once. A static RAG pipeline retrieves what you ask for. An agent figures out what to ask for in the first place.

Human review is not optional. Any output that could influence a trade needs an analyst to sign off before it reaches the PM. The system should make it easy to approve, push back, or ask for more detail.

Start with 15 companies, not 50. You prove it out on the core holdings first, then expand once you trust the outputs.

Stage 4

How you'd know if it's working

KPIs need to be defined before the pilot starts, not after. If you wait to see what the data shows, it's too easy to rationalize any result. These are the metrics I would commit to measuring from day one, with targets agreed upfront.

Metric What it measures Target How
Signal recall rate % of material events flagged that analysts agreed were worth surfacing 85%+ Weekly analyst review log
False positive rate % of alerts analysts dismissed as noise Under 20% One-click dismiss with optional reason
Time to first briefing Speed from material event to alert delivered Under 30 min Timestamp comparison
Analyst research time Hours per week on information gathering vs. analysis 40% reduction Self-reported timesheet vs. baseline
Briefing usage rate % of briefings analysts actually open and read 70%+ System logs
Analyst trust score Confidence in AI outputs, 1 to 5 scale 3.5+ avg Short survey every two weeks
how the pilot would run

60 days, 2 analysts, top 15 holdings. Both analysts keep doing their normal workflow alongside the AI system so you can actually compare the two. At day 30 you do a check-in. At day 60 you decide whether to expand or go back and redesign something.

One thing I would insist on before any of this starts: a baseline week where analysts track exactly how they spend their time today. Without that, the 40% reduction target is just a number nobody can verify.

Stage 5

Getting it actually used

In my experience with ALMA, the technical build is actually the easier part. Getting people to trust it and change how they work is where most of the effort goes.

Month 1-2
Pilot with 2 analysts on the top 15 holdings
The system runs alongside the existing workflow, not replacing it. Weekly feedback sessions to tune signal scoring based on what analysts are actually dismissing.
Month 3
Review what worked, fix what did not, bring in the full team
Go through the KPIs honestly. Fix the top issues. Onboard the rest of the analysts with a hands-on session using their actual companies, not a slide deck.
Month 4
Expand to the full 50+ company universe
Only happens if the pilot KPIs were actually met. New data sources like export control filings and Tier 2/3 supply chain data get added here.
Month 5-6
Monitor model health on an ongoing basis
The semiconductor landscape shifts constantly. Assign one analyst as the internal owner whose job is to flag when outputs start degrading before the rest of the team notices.
Month 6+
Report back to leadership on actual ROI
A simple dashboard showing time saved, signal recall over time, and any cases where the system surfaced something before a market move. This is how the project gets renewed.
risks worth flagging upfront
Analyst resistance
Senior analysts may feel the system threatens their role
High
Be upfront from day one that this is about saving time, not replacing anyone. Get one or two senior analysts involved in shaping the output format early so they feel ownership over it.
Hallucination on financial data
LLMs can state incorrect numbers with complete confidence
High
Every number in the output needs to be cited back to a source. This is what we learned building ALMA. You need guardrails that force the model to verify its own claims before the output goes anywhere.
Data licensing issues
Some providers prohibit feeding their content into AI models
Medium
Legal needs to review every data provider agreement before anything gets ingested. Better to find out upfront than mid-build.
Model drift over time
The semiconductor landscape changes constantly
Medium
Do a quarterly review of the prompts and signal taxonomy. Have someone whose job it is to notice when the outputs start feeling stale before the analysts lose trust in it.
Over-reliance on outputs
Analysts stop reading primary sources
Low/ongoing
Always include source links and make it easy to drill down. If analysts stop clicking through to underlying sources, that is an early warning sign.
