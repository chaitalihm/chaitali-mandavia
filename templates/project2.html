Deploying agentic AI for the semiconductor team at a long/short hedge fund

I picked one team at a large multi-sector fund. The analysts covering global semiconductors. Worked through what it would actually take to build something useful for them.

Stage 1

Why this makes economic sense

The semiconductor team covers a lot of companies across a fast-moving market. The problem isn't that the analysts are bad at their jobs — it's that there's too much to read and not enough hours to read it. That's the pitch.

50+
companies tracked across TSMC, ASML, Nvidia, SMIC, and the broader supply chain
~3 hrs
per company per week just to stay current on filings, news, and supply chain signals
$22k
estimated weekly labor cost across the team at $150/hr before any AI investment
where analyst time actually goes each week
the pitch

A long/short fund lives on information speed. When TSMC revises its capacity guidance, when ASML gets hit by a new export restriction, when a supplier goes offline, the team that knows first positions first. Right now analysts spend most of their time gathering and summarizing rather than deciding. The idea is to build something that handles the first part so they can focus on the second.

Larger funds like Millennium and Point72 have been building AI research infrastructure for a while. Bloomberg and FactSet both launched LLM tools in 2024. The question for a mid-size fund is whether off-the-shelf is enough. My view is no. Generic tools don't understand the relationship between something like ASML shipment delays and TSMC's 3nm yield trajectory. That specificity only comes from something built for this team specifically.

what I would recommend

Build a custom agentic monitoring system for the semiconductor team specifically. Start with the top 15 holdings, get it working well there, then expand. Going broad too early is how these projects fall apart.

Stage 2

Questions I'd ask before building anything

The number one reason AI projects fail is that nobody spent enough time with the actual users before building. I would want to sit down with the analysts and the CIO before touching any code. These are the questions I would ask.

Coverage and data
1What does your coverage universe look like? Full list, how you'd bucket them by importance, and which 10 companies — if you missed something — would hurt the most.
2Where does your information come from today? Every source an analyst touches in a typical week — terminals, filings, supply chain data providers, broker reports, export control filings.
3What do you have API access to, and what requires a human to go find it?
Workflow and pain points
4Walk me through a typical Monday morning. What do you read, in what order, and what do you produce by end of day?
5What's the most time-consuming thing that adds the least value? Synthesizing earnings transcripts? Tracking supply chain signals? Writing first drafts of research notes?
6Tell me about a specific time you were late on something that mattered. What happened, and what would have needed to be different?
7What signals have historically driven your best trades? Capacity utilization shifts? Lead time changes? Export license approvals? Management tone on earnings calls?
Technical and org
8What are your data residency and compliance requirements? Restrictions on sending information to third-party APIs?
9Where would analysts actually want to see this? In their existing tools, a new dashboard, a Slack digest, email?
10Who owns the decision to adopt this? Who has veto power?
Stage 3

What the system actually looks like

An orchestrator agent runs on a schedule, pulls from multiple sources, extracts what matters, and routes it to the right analyst with context. No voice, no computer vision — this is a text-heavy information synthesis problem.

how the system is structured
key decisions

Why agentic and not a simple RAG pipeline? Monitoring 50+ companies means the system needs to decide what to look for, when to dig deeper, and how to prioritize — not just retrieve what you ask for. A static pipeline retrieves. An agent figures out what to look for in the first place.

Human review is not optional. Anything that could influence a trade needs an analyst to sign off before it reaches the PM.

Start with 15 companies, not 50. Prove it works on the core holdings first, then expand.

pilot structure
how the pilot would run

60 days, 2 analysts, top 15 holdings. Both analysts keep their normal workflow running alongside the system so you can compare the two directly. Check-in at day 30, go/no-go decision at day 60.

One thing I'd insist on before anything starts: a baseline week where analysts track exactly how they spend their time. Without that, the 40% reduction target is just a number nobody can actually verify.

Stage 5

Getting it actually used

Working on ALMA taught me that the technical build is the easier part. Getting people to trust it and actually change how they work is where most of the effort goes.

Month 1-2
Pilot with 2 analysts on the top 15 holdings
System runs alongside the existing workflow. Weekly feedback sessions to tune signal scoring based on what analysts are actually dismissing as noise.
Month 3
Review what worked, fix what didn't, bring in the full team
Go through the KPIs honestly. Fix the top issues. Onboard the rest of the analysts with a hands-on session using their actual companies, not a slide deck.
Month 4
Expand to the full 50+ company universe
Only if the pilot KPIs were actually met. New data sources like export control filings and Tier 2/3 supply chain data get added here.
Month 5-6
Monitor model health
The semiconductor landscape shifts constantly. Assign one analyst as the internal owner whose job is to flag when outputs start degrading before the rest of the team notices.
Month 6+
Report back to leadership on actual ROI
A simple dashboard showing time saved, signal recall, and any cases where the system surfaced something before a market move. This is how the project gets renewed.
risks worth flagging upfront

The biggest risk is analyst resistance. Senior people may feel this threatens their role, so being upfront that it's about saving time matters a lot. Getting one or two of them involved in shaping the output format early helps — they're more likely to trust something they had a hand in building.

Hallucination is the other major one. LLMs can state incorrect numbers with complete confidence, which is especially dangerous in a financial context. Every figure in the output needs to be cited back to a source. This is something we learned building ALMA — you need guardrails that force the model to verify its own claims before anything goes out.

A few smaller things worth sorting out early: data licensing (some providers prohibit their content being fed into AI models, so legal needs to review everything before ingestion), model drift (the semiconductor landscape shifts fast, so someone needs to own a quarterly review of whether the outputs still make sense), and over-reliance (always include source links — if analysts stop clicking through, that's an early warning that they're treating AI output as ground truth).
