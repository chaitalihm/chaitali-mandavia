Deploying agentic AI at a

1
Business case

Why this makes economic sense

Before writing a line of code, you need to show the fund why this is worth doing. Semiconductor markets are a good fit for this argument because the coverage universe is wide, information moves fast, and analyst time is genuinely the bottleneck. There are only so many hours in a day and too many companies to track.

50+
companies to track across TSMC, ASML, Nvidia, SMIC, and the broader supply chain
~3 hrs
analyst time per company per week just to stay current on filings, news, and supply chain signals
150+ hrs
weekly research hours across the team that could be partially handed off, at roughly $150/hr that is $22,500 a week in labor
The pitch

A long/short semiconductor fund lives on information speed. When TSMC revises its capacity guidance, when ASML gets hit by a new export restriction, when a supplier in Malaysia goes offline, the fund that knows first positions first. Right now analysts spend most of their time gathering and summarizing information rather than actually doing something with it.

The idea is to give the team a system that reads everything, flags what matters, and puts together a first draft so analysts can spend their time on the stuff that actually needs a human. Not to replace anyone, just to clear the noise so they can focus.

What others are doing

Larger funds like Millennium and Point72 have been investing in AI research infrastructure for a while now. Bloomberg and FactSet both launched LLM-based research tools in 2024. The question for a mid-size fund is whether off-the-shelf tools are enough or whether a custom build makes more sense.

My view is that generic tools cover general market news fine, but they do not understand the relationship between something like ASML shipment delays and TSMC's 3nm yield trajectory. That kind of domain-specific signal is only possible with something purpose-built for this coverage universe.

What I would recommend

Build a custom agentic monitoring system designed around the semiconductor supply chain specifically. Start with the top 15 holdings, prove it out, then expand. Going broad too early is how these projects fall apart.

2
Discovery

Questions I'd ask before building anything

The number one reason AI deployments fail is that nobody spent enough time understanding the actual workflow before building anything. I would want to sit down with the analysts and the CIO before touching any code. These are the questions I would ask.

Coverage and data
01
What does your coverage universe actually look like? Give me the full list — names, tickers, how you'd bucket them by importance.
Which 10 companies, if you missed something on them, would cost you the most?
Are there any companies where you feel like you're consistently late?
02
Where does your information come from today? Walk me through every source an analyst touches in a typical week.
Bloomberg/Reuters/FactSet terminals?
Company filings (10-K, 20-F, earnings transcripts)?
Supply chain data providers — TrendForce, IDC, Gartner?
Export control and regulatory filings (BIS, EU)?
Channel checks, broker reports, expert network calls?
03
What data do you have API or programmatic access to, and what requires a human to go find it?
Current workflow and pain points
04
Walk me through what an analyst does on a typical Monday morning. Specifically: what do they read, in what order, and what do they produce by end of day?
05
What's the most time-consuming thing analysts do that they feel adds the least value?
Synthesizing earnings transcripts across multiple companies?
Tracking supply chain signals from secondary sources?
Writing up the first draft of a research note?
06
Can you tell me about a specific time you were late on something that mattered? What happened, and what would have needed to be different for you to catch it earlier?
07
What does a good research output look like for your team? Is it a one-pager? A model update? A verbal briefing to the PM?
Investment process and decision-making
08
How do new position ideas actually get generated? Is it bottom-up from analysts, top-down from the PM, or both?
09
What signals have historically driven your best trades in this sector? I'm asking because the AI should be optimized to surface those specifically.
Capacity utilization shifts at foundries?
Lead time changes for capital equipment?
Export license approvals or denials?
Management tone shifts across earnings calls?
10
How much do you trust AI-generated outputs today? What would an AI need to get right consistently before you'd act on it?
Technical and compliance constraints
11
What are your data residency and compliance requirements? Does firm data need to stay in a specific region? Any restrictions on sending information to third-party APIs?
12
What internal tools does the team actually use day-to-day? Where would they want to see AI output — in their existing workflow, or a new interface?
Bloomberg terminal integration?
Slack or Teams notifications?
A standalone dashboard?
Email digest?
13
Who owns the decision to adopt this internally? Is it the CIO, Head of Research, or someone else? Who has veto power?
3
Architecture

What the system actually looks like

Based on what the discovery conversations would surface, here is the architecture I would go with. The core idea is an orchestrator agent that runs on a schedule, pulls from multiple data sources, extracts structured signals, and routes them to the right analyst with context. No voice, no computer vision, this is a text-heavy information synthesis problem.

Inputs
Filings & Transcripts
SEC, 20-F, earnings calls via API
News & Wire
Reuters, Bloomberg, Nikkei feeds
Supply Chain Data
TrendForce, IDC, lead time trackers
Regulatory
BIS export controls, EU/US policy filings
↓
Agent
Orchestrator Agent
Runs on schedule, routes tasks to sub-agents, manages context window
Signal Extractor
Identifies material events, scores by relevance to coverage universe
RAG Layer
Retrieves historical context from vector store to ground summaries
↓
Outputs
Daily Briefing
Ranked digest of material events by company and signal type
Alert System
Real-time push for high-urgency events above threshold
Research Draft
First-pass write-up with cited sources for analyst review
Key decisions

Why agentic and not a simple RAG pipeline? Monitoring 50+ companies requires the system to decide what to look for, when to dig deeper on a signal, and how to prioritize across the whole coverage universe at once. A static RAG pipeline retrieves what you ask for. An agent figures out what to ask for in the first place.

Human review is not optional. Any output that could influence a trade needs an analyst to sign off before it reaches the PM. The system should make it easy to approve, push back, or ask for more detail, not just passively consume whatever the AI produces.

Start with 15 companies, not 50. Going broad too early is how these projects fall apart. You prove it out on the core holdings first, then expand once you trust the outputs.

4
Pilot and KPIs

How you'd know if it's working

You need to decide what success looks like before the pilot starts, not after. If you wait to see what the data shows, it is too easy to rationalize any result. These are the metrics I would commit to measuring from day one, with targets agreed upfront.

Metric What it measures Target at 90 days How to measure
Signal recall rate % of material events the system flagged that analysts agreed were worth flagging 85%+ Weekly analyst review log, asking whether they agreed the signal was worth surfacing
False positive rate % of alerts that analysts dismissed as noise Under 20% Same review log, one-click dismiss with optional reason
Time to first briefing How fast after a material event the system surfaces it Under 30 min Timestamp when event appeared in source vs. when alert was delivered
Analyst research time Hours per week spent gathering information vs. actually analyzing it 40% reduction Self-reported weekly timesheet compared to pre-pilot baseline
Briefing usage rate % of generated briefings that analysts actually open and read 70%+ System logs: opened, time spent, actions taken
Analyst trust score How confident analysts feel in the AI outputs on a 1 to 5 scale 3.5+ avg Short survey every two weeks, tracked over time
How the pilot would run

60 days, 2 analysts, top 15 holdings. Both analysts keep doing their normal workflow alongside the AI system so you can actually compare the two. At day 30 you do a check-in. At day 60 you decide whether to expand or go back and redesign something.

One thing I would insist on before any of this starts: a baseline week where analysts track exactly how they spend their time today. Without that, the 40% reduction target is just a number nobody can verify.

5
Deployment and beyond

Getting it actually used

In my experience with ALMA, the technical build is actually the easier part. Getting people to trust it and change how they work is where most of the effort goes. This stage is about that.

Month 1-2
Pilot with 2 analysts on the top 15 holdings
The system runs alongside the existing workflow, not replacing it. Weekly feedback sessions to figure out what is getting dismissed as noise and tune the signal scoring accordingly.
Month 3
Review what worked, fix what did not, bring in the full team
Go through the KPIs honestly. Fix the top issues from the pilot. Onboard the rest of the analysts with a hands-on session using their actual companies, not a slide deck.
Month 4
Expand to the full 50+ company universe
Only happens if the pilot KPIs were actually met. Add new data sources here too, things like export control filings and deeper supply chain data from Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers.
Month 5-6
Monitor model health on an ongoing basis
The semiconductor landscape shifts constantly so you need someone checking whether the system is keeping up. Assign one analyst as the internal owner whose job is to flag when things start degrading.
Month 6+
Report back to leadership on what the ROI actually was
A simple dashboard showing time saved, signal recall over time, and any cases where the system surfaced something before a market move. This is how the project gets renewed and built out further.
Risks I'd flag upfront
Analyst resistance
Senior analysts may feel the system is threatening their role or signaling future headcount cuts
High
Be upfront from day one that this is about saving time, not replacing anyone. Get one or two senior analysts involved in shaping the output format early so they feel ownership over it.
Hallucination on financial data
LLMs can state incorrect numbers from earnings or filings with complete confidence
High
Every number in the output needs to be cited back to a source. This is actually what we learned building ALMA too. You need guardrails that force the model to verify its own claims and provide specific citations before the output goes anywhere.
Data licensing issues
Some data providers explicitly prohibit feeding their content into AI models
Medium
Legal needs to review every data provider agreement before anything gets ingested. Some will need separate AI licensing and that costs money. Better to find out upfront than mid-build.
Model drift over time
The semiconductor landscape changes constantly, new players, new supply chains, new geopolitical dynamics
Medium
Do a quarterly review of the prompts and signal taxonomy. Have someone whose job it is to notice when the outputs start feeling stale or off before the analysts start losing trust in it.
Over-reliance on outputs
Analysts stop reading primary sources and just defer to whatever the AI surfaces
Low/ongoing
Always include source links in the output and make it easy to drill down. If analysts stop clicking through to the underlying sources, that is an early sign something is going wrong.
