What even is an LLM?

By Chaitali Mandavia June 2025 5 min read

You've probably typed something into one of them, like ChatGPT, or Claude, or Gemini. But if someone asked you right now to explain what it actually is, you might find yourself saying something like "it's an AI" and that's fine.

LLM stands for Large Language Model. Let's break it down.

Model

A lot of people assume an AI like ChatGPT works like a very fast search engine where when you ask something, it looks it up, and it tells you. But that's not what is happening, the model is actually generating words.

In the machine learning sense, a model is something that has been trained to recognize patterns. Once you show it enough examples of something, it will build an internal understanding of the rules, even if nobody explicitly programmed those rules in.

an analogy that might help

Think about how a kid learns language. Nobody sits them down and teaches them grammar rules first, rather they just hear millions of sentences over years and start building an internal sense of what sounds right. An LLM learns the same way, except instead of years, it's trained on a large amount of text in a short time, and instead of a brain, it's a neural network.

So what is it actually doing?

When you type something into ChatGPT, the model takes your input and starts predicting what word should come next, and the words after. The whole response is built one word at a time, and each word is chosen based on what's most likely to follow everything that came before it.

The "large" part is that the model has been trained on a huge amount of text including books, websites, articles, code, conversations. During training, it builds up a massive web of connections between words, concepts, and ideas. For example, it learns that "Paris" and "France" tend to appear together, or that "diagnosis" usually follows medical language.

The web of connections

The more you train it, the better these connections get. Early in training, a model might know that "dog" and "cat" are both animals, but it might not understand for example, that a question about a "bank" near a "river" probably means the riverbank and not a financial institution. The more examples it sees, the more nuanced its internal map of language becomes.

Context window

One thing worth knowing is that an LLM can only "see" a certain amount of text at once and this is called the context window. Kind of like the LLM has short-term memory where everything inside the window, the model can work with, however everything outside it, it has no idea about.

This is why if you have a very long conversation with ChatGPT, the responses can start to feel a little off toward the end because it literally no longer has access to what was said earlier. This is also the reason something called RAG exists, which I'll cover in the next post.

Why it sometimes makes things up

One of the key problems is that LLMs can be confidently wrong. The technical term is hallucination. Since the model is always predicting the most plausible next word, sometimes it generates something that sounds right but isn't. It's just pattern-matching, and sometimes the most statistically likely sequence of words happens to be incorrect.

the practical takeaway

Always verify anything an LLM gives you that actually matters. This is why for most enterprise tasks we account for a human fallback rate where some percentage of work is routed back to humans since it's either too complex or needs verification. The model has no idea when it's wrong, so it won't flag it for you.

The short version

An LLM is a machine learning model trained on a huge amount of text. It learns patterns like which words and ideas tend to go together, how sentences are structured, how questions get answered. When you give it a prompt, it generates a response word by word based on those patterns. The more training, the better the web of connections, the more useful the output.
