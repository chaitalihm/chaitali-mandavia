What is a token, really? Why "unbelievable" is three pieces, not one

By Chaitali Mandavia 2025 6 min read

When people talk about how much something costs to run on an LLM, or why a long prompt is slower than a short one, what they're really talking about is tokens. I used to assume a model reads words the way I do. It actually reads tokens, and a token is usually a piece of a word.

Why isn't every word just one token?

My first assumption was that a model would just have a dedicated slot for every English word. It would make sense, except English alone has well over 170,000 words, plus every other language, every technical term, every name people use. If every single word got its own slot, the model's vocabulary would balloon into millions of entries, which makes everything bigger and slower to run.

So instead, models use a smaller vocabulary, usually somewhere between 50,000 and 100,000 pieces, and build longer or rarer words out of smaller, reusable fragments. The word "unbelievable" might get split into three pieces:

how "unbelievable" actually gets tokenized
un believ able
1 word → 3 tokens

How does it decide where to split?

This is the part I found genuinely surprising. It's not a person deciding "un" means negation and "able" means capability. The vocabulary is built automatically using a process that just looks at which letter combinations show up together most often across an enormous amount of text, and merges those into single units. Roughly:

1. Start by breaking everything into individual letters
  "unbelievable" → u-n-b-e-l-i-e-v-a-b-l-e
2. Find the letter pairs that show up together most often across the training text, merge them
  "a" + "b" appears constantly → merge into "ab"
3. Keep repeating this, thousands of times, building up bigger and bigger common chunks
  eventually "able" and "un" each earn their own token
4. Whatever chunks are left over once you run out of common merges become the final tokens

So "able" earns a slot in the vocabulary because that exact sequence of letters showed up together constantly during training. The fact that it happens to mean something to an English speaker is basically a coincidence of how often it appeared, nobody sat down and decided "able" deserved its own token because of what it means.

what this means for a word with no real roots

Take a word like "xylophone." It has no useful prefix or suffix to split on, but the model will still chop it up into whatever chunks happened to be common during training. Maybe "xyl" and "ophone," maybe something else entirely, depending on what showed up frequently. The split is just a record of what letter sequences were statistically common during training. It has no relationship to what the word actually means.

Why this actually matters: tokens cost compute

Here's the part that connects directly to why companies charge per token, and why a long prompt is measurably slower and more expensive than a short one. Every single token a model processes has to go through the model's full pipeline, the embedding lookup, every layer of matrix multiplication, all of it. More tokens simply means more math.

This isn't a rough approximation, it's close to exact. The amount of computation a model does scales directly with how many tokens you give it. The arithmetic intensity of a matrix multiplication is roughly equal to the batch size, where batch size really just means how many tokens are being processed at once. Double the tokens, double the math. Ten times the tokens, ten times the math.

why a longer prompt actually costs more compute

There's one more wrinkle worth knowing. The tokens the model generates in its response tend to be more expensive than the tokens in your prompt. Part of the reason is structural: moving data between the chip's memory and its compute cores has to happen for every token, and generation happens one token at a time in sequence, while your input prompt can be processed all at once, in parallel. That sequential nature of generation is part of why API pricing almost always charges more per output token than per input token, the output side is doing more relative work per token.

the practical takeaway

When you're estimating cost for an AI deployment, the lever you're actually pulling is token count. A wordy, repetitive prompt costs more than a tight one because it has more tokens running through the same pipeline. The model isn't "thinking harder" about a longer prompt, it's just doing more of the same fixed math, over and over, for every extra token. This is also why summarizing a long document before passing it to a model, rather than pasting the whole thing in, can meaningfully cut cost without losing what actually matters.

The compute scaling explanation here draws on the rooflines framework from "How To Scale Your Model," Austin et al., Google DeepMind, 2025, which goes much deeper into exactly how token count, hardware FLOPs, and memory bandwidth interact if you want the full technical picture.
